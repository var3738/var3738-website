VAR REPORT ON BY-ELECTIONS HELD IN KENYA ON 27TH OCTOBER 2025
Observer: The Viable Alternative Republic (VAR)

1. Introduction and Scope of Observation
The Viable Alternative Republic (VAR) deployed observers to monitor the by-elections held in
Baringo County, Embu county, Nyamira County and Bungoma County. The observation focused on
the
electoral process before, during, and immediately after polling day, with particular attention to
electoral administration, security, political conduct, voter participation, and compliance with
constitutional and legal standards governing elections in Kenya.
VAR’s observation methodology included:


Physical presence at selected polling stations and tallying centres;



Engagement with voters, party agents, election officials, and security officers;



Direct observation of polling, counting, and tallying procedures; and



Review of publicly available statements from the Independent Electoral and Boundaries
Commission (IEBC), security agencies, political actors, and other observer missions.

VAR deployed three (3) observers in Baringo County, one (1) observer in Mbeere North, two (2)
observers in Nyamira County (Nyamaiya Ward), and one (1) observer in Bungoma County
(Kabuchai Ward). The observation focused on pre-election preparedness, polling day activities,
counting and tallying, security
arrangements, political conduct, and adherence to constitutional and legal standards. This
report reflects VAR’s independent, non-partisan assessment of the electoral process.

2. How the IEBC Handled the Election
2.1 Administrative Preparedness and Logistics
The IEBC demonstrated adequate administrative preparedness in all by election centres. Most
polling stations observed by VAR opened with essential materials such as ballot papers, ballot
boxes, registers, and results forms available. Polling staff generally appeared trained and
familiar with procedures.

5

In Baringo, logistical arrangements were largely smooth, with fewer reported delays in the
opening of polling stations. In Nyamira, however, there were isolated instances of delayed
openings, mainly attributed to security disruptions and heightened tension in certain wards
rather than logistical failure by IEBC. However, IEBC’s effectiveness was constrained by
disruptions arising from violence and political interference.
2.2 Polling, Counting, and Tallying Procedures
VAR observers noted that voting procedures—identification of voters, issuance of ballots,
secrecy of the vote, and counting—were generally conducted in accordance with the law in
stations that were not disrupted by violence.
Counting was conducted openly in the presence of agents and observers where security
conditions allowed. However, in some Nyamira stations, security incidents interfered with
continuity, causing temporary suspension or hurried conclusion of polling and counting
processes.However, the ward tallying centre, Nyamaiya Secondary was secured and counting
ana announcement of results was done peacfefully. Additionally, VAR also noted that in
Baringo, tallying centers had not received any information with regards to vote counting from
polling stations, as late as 7pm. Even more discouraging, as late as 1am, there was still
inactivity around tallying centers in Baringo. This affected the overall transparency of the
process in those areas
In Mbeere north, the polling was seamless except for Siakago social hall where violence was
witnessed. This stemmed from some agents donning party colours and clashing of supporters
when their candidates visited the polling center. The constituency tallying centre at St Bakhita
Siakago girls was well secured and tallying was done peacefully and orderly even though it
went deep into the morning of the next day after the by elections
2.3 Institutional Response to Challenges
IEBC publicly condemned incidents of violence and interference and maintained that elections
could only be credible if conducted in a peaceful environment. While the Commission acted
within its mandate, VAR observed that IEBC’s capacity to decisively prevent disruptions was
limited, as security enforcement falls outside its direct control.

3. Agent Participation
3.1 Presence of Political Party and Candidate Agents
VAR observed that political party and candidate agents were present in a majority of polling
stations, particularly in Baringo County. Agents generally understood their roles, including
observing voting, witnessing counting, and signing result forms.
In Mbeere north, Agents participated adequately in the polling and tallying even observing as IEBC
6

staff helped disenfranchised voters to cast their ballots.
In Nyamira, agent participation was uneven. While agents were present at the start of polling in
many stations, violent incidents and heightened insecurity caused:


Temporary withdrawal of agents from some stations;



Restricted access to tallying areas during security interventions; and



Increased tension between rival agents, sometimes escalating into confrontation.

7

3.2 Impact on Transparency
Where agents were able to remain throughout polling and counting, transparency was
strengthened. However, VAR notes that disruption of agent participation directly undermines
confidence in the electoral outcome, especially in areas where agents could not consistently
observe tallying.

4. Assessment of Whether the Voting Was Free and Fair
4.1 Freedom of the Vote
In Baringo County and Mbeere north, VAR observed that voting was largely peaceful, and
voters were generally able to cast their ballots without overt intimidation. However, voter
turnout was relatively low and from the elderly part of the population, raising concerns about
political disengagement rather than coercion.
In Nyamira County, particularly in some wards, the environment was not consistently
conducive to free voting. Incidents of violence, intimidation, voter bribery and road blockades
created fear among voters and, in some cases, deterred turnout. VAR notes that the presence of
fear—regardless of whether every voter is directly affected—compromises the freedom of an
election.
4.2 Fairness and Equality
Allegations of vote buying and undue influence were reported to VAR observers, especially in
high-competition areas. While not all allegations could be independently verified, their
prevalence indicates systemic challenges to electoral fairness.
VAR concludes that while the legal and procedural framework existed to deliver a fair election,
conditions on the ground in parts of Nyamira undermined that framework, resulting in an
uneven electoral playing field. While procedural standards were met in some areas, the
elections did not consistently meet the constitutional threshold of being free, fair, and credible
across all observed locations.

5. Role of the Police and Security Agencies
5.1 Security Deployment
Security agencies were deployed extensively in the counties. Police presence was visible at
polling stations, during transportation of materials, and at tallying centres. In Baringo, security
deployment was largely preventive and non-intrusive.
8

In Nyamira, police involvement was reactive and intensive, responding to outbreaks of violence,
destruction of property, and obstruction of polling.
5.2 Conduct of Security Officers
VAR observed that police generally acted to restore order and protect electoral materials.
However:


Some security interventions disrupted voting processes;



In certain instances, voters and observers experienced restricted access during security
operations; and



The reliance on force highlighted gaps in early conflict prevention and intelligence-led
policing.

Overall, VAR finds that while security agencies played a necessary role, security responses alone
cannot compensate for political intolerance and pre-election tensions.

6. Political Involvement, Chaos, and Interference
VAR observed that political competition was a major driver of instability, particularly in
Nyamira County. Indicators of political interference included:


Mobilization of supporters in ways that escalated tensions;



Confrontations between rival camps near polling stations;



Destruction of property and obstruction of movement; and



Allegations of direct or indirect incitement by political actors.

Such conduct is inconsistent with constitutional principles of peaceful political participation and
directly undermines public trust in elections.
Additionally, in Mbeere north, violence was witnessed when supporters clashed after their
candidates casted their ballots. Chaos erupted also when agents clashed but it was quelled by the
police and IEBC officials.
VAR observed politically driven violence, voter bribery, intimidation of voters, agents, and
observers, and allegations of ballot stuffing across all the counties. Such conduct was largely
attributed to rival political actors and significantly undermined public confidence in the electoral
process. VAR emphasizes that political actors bear primary responsibility for creating an
environment conducive to free and fair elections.

7. Conclusion

10

The Baringo, Mbeere north and Nyamira by-elections present a mixed electoral outcome.
From an administrative perspective, the IEBC demonstrated competence in organizing and
managing the electoral process. Where peace prevailed, voting and counting were conducted
professionally and transparently.
However, electoral integrity cannot be measured by procedure alone. Violence, intimidation,
vote-buying allegations, and political interference—particularly in Nyamira—significantly
compromised the credibility of the elections in affected areas.
VAR concludes that while the elections met minimum procedural standards in some locations,
they fell short of the constitutional ideal of elections that are universally free, fair, peaceful,
and credible.

8. General Recommendations
8.1 To the IEBC
1. Enhance coordination with security agencies during by-elections, particularly in
historically volatile wards.
2. Strengthen early-warning mechanisms to anticipate disruptions.
3. Improve voter engagement strategies to address low turnout in by-elections.
8.2 To Security Agencies
4. Shift from reactive to preventive security deployment using intelligence and community
engagement.
5. Ensure security operations do not unnecessarily restrict access for observers, agents,
and voters.
8.3 To Political Parties and Candidates
6. Commit publicly and practically to peaceful campaigns and elections.
7. Sanction supporters and affiliates involved in violence, bribery, or intimidation.
8.4 To Civil Society and Observer Groups (Including VAR)
8. Expand domestic observation coverage, particularly in high-risk areas.
9. Strengthen post-election reporting and documentation to support accountability and
electoral reforms.

9.2 Related recommendations
A. Voter Registration and Participation
1 .Introduce door-to-door voter registration similar to a national census.
2 .Recognize possession of a national ID as sufficient proof of voter registration upon attaining
18 years.
3 .Allow voters to vote at any polling station near their residence while retaining
original registration details.
4 .Deploy voter registration caravans to mobilize and register voters at the grassroots level.
5 .Establish a prize fund for registered civic movements to incentivize voter registration.
B. Voter Education, Sensitization, and Peacebuilding
1 .Conduct vigorous online voter sensitization campaigns powered by VAR.
2 .Develop and circulate an animated voter education guide nationwide.
3 .Organize quarterly peace festivals throughout 2026 led by IEBC in partnership with VAR.
4 .Establish peace runs in partnership with institutions that champion marathons for
social causes.
C. Institutional Reform and Accountability
1 .Outsource IEBC training to comparative jurisdictions with proven records of free and
fair elections.
2 Establish an independent accountability body involving LSK, faith-based organizations,
businesses, and other stakeholders.
3 Require all candidates to sign binding peace agreements with enforceable sanctions, including
disqualification upon substantiated violations.
